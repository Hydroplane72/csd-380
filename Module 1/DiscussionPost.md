Limit Work In Processes (WIP).

Article 1: "Putting the 'flow' back in workflow with WIP limits"
URL: https://www.atlassian.com/agile/kanban/wip-limits
Summary: This article argues that limiting the number of items in progress at any given time helps teams focus on completing tasks. If you look at the article, it actually gives 4 "key takeaways" at the very beginning that summarize the points it's trying to make. The article's key point is that WIP reduces multitasking.

Article 2: WIP Limits Explained: Control Flow & Reduce Lead Time
URL: https://www.learnleansigma.com/lean-manufacturing/wip-limits-explained
Summary: This article is the next step in implementing WIP limits. What actions can you take to follow through? There is a previous article on WIP limits that goes into more detail, but this article focuses on the practical application of WIP limits.  This article provides a helpful "action plan" for implementing WIP limits. 

These steps are:
1. Visualize your current workflow
 Basically, draw out your current workflow and create "columns" for each stage a task must go through to be completed.
2. Find your bottlenecks
 This is about finding the stage(s) with the most WIP items.
3. Set a Large WIP limit
 The article suggests starting with a WIP limit that is lower than the current WIP but not so low as to disrupt current processes. Instead, before starting anything new, complete current work and then keep reducing that WIP limit until you find that the team is finishing work while also not going stir crazy with too little work to do.
4. Find the "slow" columns
 This is about identifying the workflow stages that take the longest to complete tasks. This is usually easier to find when there are fewer WIP items in the workflow. If the bottleneck is bad enough, all of your WIP items will be in that stage and stuck with the same description, "Blocked by X," and "X" will be the same for all of those items. 


Opinion of the most important point:
While the point that WIP limits reduce multitasking is important, the second article's suggestions for implementing WIP limits are more important. It is always better to have a solution to a problem than just knowing there is one. The first article does a good job of explaining the problem and why WIP limits are important. Still, the second article provides actionable steps for implementing WIP limits.
